Large Language Model — From Scratch

I set out to build my own LLM so I could understand every layer between raw text and generated thought. On 1 November 2024 I opened a blank folder, installed PyTorch, and started.


The Architecture

I wrote CustomGPT: 12 decoder layers, 12 heads, 768-dim embeddings, plus a sinusoidal positional encoder. Writing attention, feed-forward, and layer norms from scratch forced me to internalize why each tensor reshape exists—and why half of debugging is catching a silent dimension mismatch.


The Data & Tokenization

I pulled 20 GB of OpenWebText and fed it through the GPT-2 tokenizer. Tokenization felt trivial until a rogue UTF-8 byte crashed the batch loader; lesson learned: preprocessing is as mission-critical as model math.


The Training Loop

Mixed-precision with torch.cuda.amp, cyclical LR, gradient clipping, and on-interrupt checkpoints keep my RTX 3060 alive. A tiny 8-sample mini-batch inches loss downward while a dynamic LR tweak nudges learning rate based on consecutive loss trends.


Interactive Chat

“Converse” mode keeps a rolling 5-turn context, streams tokens live, and logs every dialogue. Watching my own model fumble, recover, then surprise me was the moment theory became reality.


Lessons Carved in Code

• Tokens are language atoms—mis-tokenize, poison the reaction.
• Parameters are memory—each new weight is a decision the model can now make.
• Epochs are humility—loss curves rarely behave, but they always teach.
• Loss is honesty—ignore spikes and hallucinations become habits.


The Aim of this Project

When I started this project, it was out of inspiration from DeepSeek. How they totally destroyed the limits on how they trained their model. So my goal was to create an LLM training script which can be used on any device, even a laptop or a PC, and train a Claude or ChatGPT level model, through an entirely new architecture.


The Problem

But I was never able to train this model because in the end, to get a machine to write, process, or think, you truly do need significant computational resources. Even if DeepSeek got 6,000,000,000$ down to 6,000,000$—it will take time before another significant advancement is made. Maybe once again from China.

The Three Orbits

Why I Wrote This Book

I am young and impatient. The Three Orbits is my response to the systems that shape our world without our consent. By mapping those systems into concentric circles—core principles, structural engines, and what binds it—I invite readers to examine where they stand and how they might redraw the lines.


Education — The Mold

Our first orbit challenges the classroom that confuses obedience for learning. Schools reward memorization, smother curiosity, and stretch childhood across fifteen exhausting years. I offer a blueprint for mastery-based, curiosity-driven learning that abolishes rote grades and measures growth by depth of understanding, not hours on a timetable.


Technology — The Chip

Automation now targets cognition itself. I examine large language models, AI-powered economies, and the danger of hollowing out entire career pipelines. Technology is neutral; its moral weight depends on whether it amplifies human potential or replaces it. The answer lies in balancing AI assistance with pathways that still grow real expertise.


Economics — The Exchange

Scarcity has become a business model. Current markets extract value upstream and drip it downstream. I propose a multi-dimensional value system—circulating mutual benefit, decoupling survival from employment, and recognizing contributions that money overlooks. In short: economies should measure flourishing, not just profit.


Governance — The Shift

Power concentrates, then calcifies. From electoral theater to invisible empire, I identify design flaws that let authority serve itself. My fix redistributes decision-making, treats necessities as rights, and drafts a pathway toward border-transcending governance where protection never equals control.


Corruption & Conflict — The Blood

When justice falters, systems weaponize fear and nationalism. I dissect how oppression is justified, how enemies are manufactured, and how geopolitical chess turns civilians into pawns. Exposing corruption is the first step; rebuilding trust is the second—and harder—task I set before us.


Humanity — The Speech

Beneath every law and ledger beats a human heart. This section shifts from analysis to exhortation: a call for courage, empathy, and relentless truth-telling. Systems are indifferent; people are not. If we reclaim our moral agency, structures must bend or break.


The TTO Framework

At the book’s center is the Three-Orbit Framework:

• The Core — Justice, Freedom, Truth, Knowledge.
• The Structure — Education, Politics, Technology, Faith.
• The Outer Circle — The force that binds principles to practice.

Remove any element and the system collapses; honor all three layers and society approaches genuine flourishing. And if an element is removed, the system will surely thrive—but the people will suffer.


Looking Forward

These pages are not an endpoint but an invitation. Test the framework, challenge it, refine it—and above all, refuse to accept "How it is." If even one reader closes this book ready to rebuild a broken orbit, its mission is already underway.

NeuroNode

I created a startup, NeuroNode. Removing the heavy lifting between an idea and the compute it needs. Instead of spinning up VMs or containers, developers simply import raw horsepower whenever code demands it. The result is instant, millisecond-billed access to CPUs, GPUs, RAM, and NPUs.


Where the Concept Began

The trigger moment was a two-day slog to containerize a GPU training pipeline. One broken dependency forced me to rebuild an environment from scratch, even though the only thing I truly needed was forty minutes of GPU math. In that frustration I sketched the outline of VPaaT — Virtual Processing as a Service. Instead of renting whole machines, a developer would purchase exactly the compute slices a single function requires, then call them directly in code.


NeuroNode Process

Process is the working embodiment of VPaaT. I import a module called neuronode.vpaat, obtain a resource ID like gpu_xc23, and pass that object a script path. The script runs remotely and streams results back into my local scope. No virtual machine spins up, no container is built, and I never think about operating systems.


Hestia

Compute is only half of modern work; the other half is intelligence. Hestia is my response to that need. It is a tag-based orchestration layer that lets me plug GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, DeepSeek, and future models into one canvas. Every model reply is wrapped in explicit tags such as <chat> or <image>, so I can capture, remix, or pipe those blocks into tooling without guesswork.


Labs

To bring code, compute, and AI under one roof, I built Labs — a cloud IDE where VPaaT resources sit beside a console window and a snippets marketplace. I can open a Fast language project, allocate two cloud GPUs, and delegate refactoring to a Hestia preset, all within a single browser tab.


Join

While remote compute is powerful, many of my experiments still run best on hardware I own: an idle desktop, a studio laptop, or even a phone’s NPU. Join registers those local devices into a shared workspace so their resources become part of a mesh compute pool. Whether I am batch-processing photos or benchmarking an LLM fine-tune, I can pour the workload across every spare CPU and GPU in the room.


Fast

Some tasks demand total control at metal speed, so I wrote Fast — a compressed systems language where print("hello") shortens to p:hello. It keeps syntax lean, performance high, and drops directly into VPaaT objects when every microsecond counts.


Real-World Friction Removed

Since shifting my own workloads to NeuroNode I have watched environment setup times fall from tens of minutes to under two, and GPU utilization climb because nothing sits idle waiting for a container heartbeat. More important, I now explore prototypes the moment curiosity strikes: an architecture daylight simulation at breakfast, a physics solver after lunch, or a photographic style-transfer batch in the evening.


Looking Forward

I have submitted NeuroNode to the Y Combinator S25 batch and, as of this writing, I am still waiting for a response. While that decision brews, I am refining three priorities: spot-market awareness so VPaaT resources automatically migrate to cheaper data-center inventory; a stricter tag grammar for Hestia so AI blocks feed cleanly into CI/CD pipelines; and a public preview of Join for creative teams that want cluster power without a data-center budget.


Lessons Carved in Code

If architecture taught me to design space for people, and physics taught me to respect constraint, NeuroNode has taught me to treat infrastructure like clay. Compute should bend to creative flow, not the other way around. By borrowing power instead of renting machines, I reclaim the mental bandwidth that fuels discovery — and that, above all else, is the change I hope every future user feels the first time they write import neuronode.vpaat.
